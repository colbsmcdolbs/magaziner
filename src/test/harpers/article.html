On Tilt

America’s new gambling epidemic
by Jasper Craven,
Adjust
Share

Four days after online sports betting was officially launched in New York State, I got a hot tip from a football player in Pittsburgh. It was January 2022, and Ben Roethlisberger, the lumbering thirty-nine-year-old Steelers quarterback, was telling the press that his upcoming postseason bout against fellow signal-caller Patrick Mahomes and the Kansas City Chiefs wasn’t going to end well. “We probably aren’t supposed to be here,” he said. “We’re probably not a very good football team.” A few seconds later, as if his point weren’t already clear, he added, “We don’t have a chance.” I didn’t know much about sports betting, but even I could appreciate a good opportunity.

Back then, I had never so much as participated in a fantasy league, and my family held no deep sports allegiances to speak of. I was a shameless fair-weather fan: while attending college in Boston during the marathon bombing and watching the Red Sox subsequently seal an emotional World Series victory, I decided I had found a team to root for. Then I moved to New York and bought a Yankees cap—and then a Mets one for good measure. I am much like the thinly sketched fans populating those Pepsi and Tostitos commercials that air at halftime, their skin-deep identifications exemplified by the generic football jerseys they wear, bearing no recognizable name or team. My friends who are die-hard sports fans hate me.

Yet Roethlisberger’s forecast of the Chiefs game was so blunt that, despite my total lack of interest in the game itself, I couldn’t help but place a bet. At the time, a host of sports-betting apps were being feverishly promoted to young men like me, flooding our screens with dizzying promotions for “no sweat” bets, “reload bonuses,” and “odds boosts.” I opened an account on FanDuel and bet $100 on the Chiefs—one gamble in a massive $150 million wave of bets that crested in the Empire State that inaugural weekend. I didn’t catch the game, but the Steelers lost, and I won.

Four years later, I have wagered more than $18,000 on FanDuel. The story of my spending, and burgeoning football fandom, is freakishly conventional. Really, it was FanDuel that gave me a reason to watch football. Here was a means to instantly and seamlessly experience risk, feed my ego, and even find something like occasional redemption. Sometimes, if I lose more than $100, I place a bigger bet to make up for it. This phenomenon is called “chasing your losses,” and in large doses, I’m told, it can signal a problem.

I’m genetically primed to become an addict of one kind or another. My father’s side of the family is overpopulated by men with dicey compulsions: well-meaning alcoholics, recklessly optimistic spenders, and men who skip town. My grandfather lost numerous homes owing to his spiraling spending habits, his taste for nice cars, too much bourbon, and fancy restaurants. His brother, a defrocked Catholic priest with a Cessna Skyhawk aircraft and a Lincoln Continental, was excommunicated for marrying his secretary. Another of my dad’s uncles ended it all by driving into a tree after secretly accruing gambling debt. In 1929, my great-grandfather fleeced $120,000—more than $2.2 million today—from the Boston branch of the Bank of Nova Scotia. He absconded to Honduras, where he dodged the federales and boarded a boat bound for Havana, where he was caught by the FBI. (They never found the money.)

But even if I weren’t hardwired to crave a rush, there’s a good chance I would have found my way to sports betting anyway. The Supreme Court struck down a twenty-six-year-old federal ban in 2018, and today, thirty-nine states and the District of Columbia permit the activity. Nearly half of all American men aged eighteen to forty-nine maintain an online sports-betting account, a statistic surely buttressed by a multibillion-dollar advertising blitz. Emerging research suggests that the spread of sports gambling portends a huge increase in gambling addiction, which has the highest rate of suicide of any addictive behavior.

The true scope of problem gambling, however, is frustratingly difficult to quantify, largely because there is little funding for its treatment, research, and awareness efforts. The federal government dedicates virtually no resources to such work. Instead, responsibility falls to individual states, and what has emerged is a shoddy patchwork, one that is particularly stark in the state with the longest history of gambling: Nevada.

An astonishing 15 percent of Nevadans meet the threshold for problem gambling—nearly double the national average. And while the state enjoys more than $15 billion in gambling revenue—the highest annual gross of any state in the country—it allocates a measly $2 million to addiction treatment. Some assistance comes from sportsbooks and casinos themselves, though this complicates certain priorities. Advocates are blunt about the crisis they see coming. Kobie West, the clinical director of the Dr. Robert Hunter International Problem Gambling Center, in Las Vegas, compares the present moment in gambling addiction to the days of blissful ignorance that allowed America’s opioid epidemic to spiral out of control. Both public-health crises, West argues, were fueled by rampant advertising and ease of access. He estimates that we will look back in several years’ time in horror.

Last September, I boarded a flight to Las Vegas with the aim of investigating the nature and scope of this problem where it appears most dramatically. If Vegas has become something of a metonym for the all-knowing, super-intelligent infrastructure of sports-betting platforms—where bookies have joined forces with apps and algorithms—it was also the old-growth ecosystem that sprouted all its signature thrills, and which, under the new dispensation, continues to innovate. My hope was that behind one of Sin City’s dark doors, I might find indicators of betting’s future, clues to my own confounding habit, or if nothing else, the keys to a winning streak.

When Nevada legalized sports gambling in 1949, “turf clubs,” or sportsbooks, operated outside of casinos, but this separation did not last long. They began to integrate in the mid-Seventies—a relationship that Vegas honors to this day. While I was able to launch my FanDuel career from my couch in Brooklyn, every sports bettor in this city must have their digital betting account validated by a flesh-and-blood attendant at a casino. It is somehow quaint to imagine so many online bettors trekking to their local gambling hall—generally a windowless room with cashiers, odds boards, plush couches, and an array of television screens—just to get their apps to work.

Shortly after I checked into my room at the Plaza Hotel and Casino, I ventured down Fremont Street, iPhone in hand, to a small sportsbook at the Downtown Grand. It was empty save for a wall of monitors and a Caesars associate named Nick, who wore a long-sleeve polo embossed with his employer’s gold chalice logo and rimless gold glasses like Adam Sandler’s in Uncut Gems.

Nick coolly informed me about the promotions available to new users, then went about the work of validating my license and verifying my profile via his tablet. But the Caesars app kept glitching, a symptom, Nick said, of higher-than-usual traffic caused by a big-ticket championship fight between Canelo Álvarez and Terence Crawford the following night, and the Las Vegas Raiders’ home opener two days later. As the app buffered, Nick launched into a monologue, delivered slightly sotto voce, on the vices of his adopted hometown: “Vegas is about habits. Can you handle the habits? Are you a gambler? Are you an alcoholic? Are you a drug addict? Are you a sex fiend? If you ain’t none of them, you gonna make it in Vegas. The weak can’t make it. We watch people go in and out, man.”

I asked Nick to estimate what percentage of people wash out of Vegas, and he didn’t hesitate for a second. “Shit, man, that’s gotta be like thirty percent.” A woman poked her head into the room, and Nick pivoted abruptly. “How are you doing?” he asked in a warmer register. “Are you hooked up on the app yet? Come on over here. I can help you.”

Nick’s warning, of course, is backed up by all the available evidence. In my research, I had noted the costs of gambling addiction: bankruptcy, alcohol-use disorder, intimate-partner violence, divorce, suicide. Gambling addiction is, in some sense, also especially vexing to treat. You can’t quit money cold turkey, and it looms especially large in recovery, with gobs of it needed to climb out of gambling debt and reclaim stability. These conditions threaten relapse, keeping alive the fantasy of a lucky roll in a high-stakes room. As one gambling-addiction specialist explained: “I’ve never had a late-stage alcoholic say, ‘If I get drunk just right, my liver will heal.’ ”

Once my app was activated, I walked down the street to the El Cortez, landing in a cozy sports lounge populated by a few guys drinking beers in armchairs and tapping their feet on the carpet anxiously. I reviewed a panel of TVs in front of me, then placed a series of bets at random: $10 on the Mets, $10 on the Red Sox, $10 on the Colorado Buffaloes. I would go on to lose all these bets, though I did win $5 on a German soccer match, guessing correctly that Borussia Dortmund would beat FC Heidenheim, their foes to the south.

During a commercial break in the Sox game, I shifted my attention to another set of screens, these showing twenty-four-hour horse-racing channels. One of the few men watching had a high-and-tight salt-and-pepper haircut and wore a graphic T-shirt that read hold my beer: dad’s about to do something stupid. Next to him were a couple of empty bottles of Pacifico and some betting slips. He turned out to be an attorney, so I sought his advice. He told me to put my money on Y’all Come, a horse sired, according to his online profile, by Cherokee Path and foaled by Hardly an Angel. From my layman’s perspective, this seemed promising, plus the attorney seemed invested. I put five dollars on Y’all Come to win and netted three.

As I collected my winnings at the cash-out cage, I noticed on the counter a thin stack of pamphlets about problem gambling, featuring a blazing yellow-and-purple sunset and titled when the fun stops. As I paged through a pamphlet, my email inbox was being inundated by gambling spam. Thanks, I assumed, to a data-sharing agreement between the NFL and the sportsbooks, I also received a stream of messages from the league, the first of them being an invitation to apply for an NFL credit card that, among its other perks, would qualify me for discounted tickets.

With Nick’s sermon in mind, I hit the Plaza’s desolate gym each morning and avoided most non-gambling vices. On the night of the Álvarez–Crawford fight, I ran into a guy under the Strip’s faux Statue of Liberty who was hawking promotional T-shirts and, less vocally, cocaine; I politely refused both. I didn’t know anything about boxing, but Crawford was the American, and the underdog too, so I staked $40 on his success. As swarms of fans pregamed for the fight, I popped into sportsbooks on the Strip and signed up for additional betting apps, eager to clinch their introductory offers. I quickly learned that validating my apps in casinos generated additional enticements, not just free drinks and giant live broadcasts but also, by proximity, whizzing slots and fuzzy-felt craps tables. I took it as a divine sign when, while waiting in line for the sportsbook at the pyramid-shaped Luxor Hotel, I found myself standing behind a woman whose lower back featured a tattoo of a pharaoh. A few minutes later, after signing up for the BetMGM app, I darted over to a roulette table, put $100 on red, and lost.

I woke up the next morning to good news: Crawford had won his fight. Better yet, it was now Sunday—the high holy day of football betting. At around eight o’clock, I ventured to Circa Resort, the first major casino to cater to online sports junkies. Circa boasts what it calls the “longest bar West of the Mississippi,” plus a massive theaterlike sportsbook, frequent sports-themed parties, and an in-house sports-betting radio station.

As the various NFL games kicked off, hundreds of bettors began whooping and hollering in front of Circa’s dizzying display of games. Undisturbed by this commotion were a few men dozing off in armchairs. One guy wearing a World Series of Poker cap was snoozing just a few feet from Circa’s Sports Gambling Hall of Fame, which features plaques commemorating America’s betting behemoths, including several men who live overseas for fear of prosecution. The most famous person on the wall was Billy Walters, a former car salesman whose highly secretive tactics netted him hundreds of millions of dollars, making him, by some accounts, the most successful bettor in sports history.

Circa also has an area called Stadium Swim: a complex of outdoor pools and cabanas oriented around a 143-foot-high screen that looks like the War Room bomb board in Dr. Strangelove, but geared toward betting, displaying all football games simultaneously, alongside live odds. Ear-splitting speakers blasted color commentary from the likes of Tony Romo and Tom Brady, and during commercial breaks, a DJ filled the air with remixes of songs with names like “Help Me Lose My Mind.” Enraptured by the scene, I laid $5 on the New York Liberty, $10 on the Patriots, $100 on the Bills, $200 on the Eagles, made a three- and four-leg parlay—a bet in which one predicts the realization of a linked series of sports outcomes—and placed a couple of other bets on overseas table tennis: $5 on Grzegorz Wichowski, from Poland, and $5 on Kirill Fadeev, a German left-hander.

The crowd generally consisted of an increasingly familiar strain of the male id: fratty, flagrant sorts often on the verge of apparent breakdown. I saw one guy, a stone-faced thirtysomething, wearing a sash that read same vagina forever. I saw men barking bets next to towers of lobster corn dogs, and others transfixed by bottle girls. One guy in a bathing suit and a New York Jets cap screamed, “What the fuck?!”

Mitch Jones, a twenty-nine-year-old recovering addict, told me he’d dedicated forty hours a week to sports betting for years, largely to avoid life’s routine lulls and irritations. By betting on sports he had found a way to “kill as much or as little time” as he wanted. A female bettor I met at Circa rightly noted that wagering is a universal opportunity to inject a bit of meaning and emotion into daily life. “Why not care a little bit?” she reasoned.

I approached a preternaturally calm man wearing a Colin Kaepernick jersey who gave his name only as Q. “I don’t bet,” he told me. When I asked why, his answer was simple: “I’m just not into losing money.” Q felt that sports, like so many other American institutions, had lost their integrity. He stopped watching the NFL for a year after Kaepernick’s ouster and now casts a skeptical eye on the entire enterprise. He told me he had doubts about the independence of referees and questions about how the sportsbooks set betting lines—in essence, numerical representations of the likelihood that something will happen—and spreads, Vegas-generated, highly specific, bettable margins of victory. “I don’t think everybody’s involved,” Q said. “But just enough pieces are involved to make it work.”

For most of its existence, the NFL has taken pains to avoid any open association with gambling. The truth, however, is that many of the early titans of the league were bookies, fight promoters, and gamblers, including Eagles owner Leonard Tose, who was forced to sell his team after accruing some $50 million in gambling losses, and Carroll Rosenbloom, the owner of the Los Angeles Rams and a heavy bettor whose 1979 death by drowning occurred under suspicious circumstances. Nearly four years later, in the inaugural episode of the PBS investigative series Frontline, a gambler named John Piazza infamously claimed that he had helped rig a dozen games between 1968 and 1970, with the active participation of two players and a coach. As he explained, “With the quarterback, if he knew the perimeters of the score we wanted to hold . . . he’d throw a bad pass or throw it out of bounds.”

Since the 2018 Supreme Court decision, at least a dozen professional football players have been suspended over illicit betting, and nearly every other sports league has seen similar flare-ups of illegal activity, including a sprawling case, unveiled late last year by the Department of Justice, involving major figures in the NBA. Among those charged was the former Cleveland Cavaliers guard Damon Jones, who, according to the feds, leaked inside information gleaned from his role as an unofficial assistant coach for the Los Angeles Lakers. Where some players are covertly betting, others are facing threats from bettors who lose money on their performance.

I learned of these threats and allegations, it’s worth noting, through sports-media outlets that are themselves overloaded with sports-gambling content and advertisements, most notably ESPN, which offers fantasy leagues and a betting platform (formerly ESPN BET, which the network has since shuttered in favor of a partnership with DraftKings). For decades, betting lines sometimes appeared alongside colorful game recaps in the newspaper; now most sports outlets openly drive readers to betting apps and are littered with articles that focus on numbers over narrative. In the years before online sports betting was legalized, hardcore bettors voraciously sought quality intel via “sports advisers” and professional handicappers like Stu “The Source” Feiner, who reportedly charged clients $999 a year for game advice that yielded decidedly mixed results. Fittingly, he’s now a personality affiliated with the Barstool Sports empire. Today, everyone is making predictions: seemingly the entire lineup of any network’s stable of sports pundits, social media influencers, and podcasters, as well as former athletes like Rob Gronkowski, a FanDuel spokesperson whose public persona hinges on the perception that he’s a dimwit.

As the hot Vegas morning creeped into a shadowy afternoon, I watched ecstatically as my bets paid off. The Liberty won, the Bills won, the Eagles won, and then, to clinch a crucial parlay, the Dallas Cowboys won, first by sending the game into overtime with a mammoth sixty-four-yard field goal kick, then securing victory with another three points. Ahead of both kicks, I nervously jostled a lucky pair of Bellagio dice around in my pocket—asking for good wind and having my prayers answered. (The Apple Watch I was wearing logged my heart rate at 167 bpm, approaching the upper limit for a man running a marathon.) Meanwhile, a bettor next to me freaked out as the Miami Dolphins quarterback Tua Tagovailoa blew his lead to the Patriots, a team I had bet on to win.

“Fly-ass pass,” the guy grumbled.

“Too many concussions,” reasoned another bettor.

The next day, having cashed out, I headed toward the stadium where the Raiders would play the Los Angeles Chargers. Over the course of my walk, I whipped myself up to go big. I had already placed about $100 on the Raiders winning, but on my path to the stadium, I passed some of the Strip’s high-class outlets, including an authorized Rolex dealer. I had been pining for an Oystersteel Datejust with a fluted bezel since the early days of the pandemic and, after some unhinged back-of-the-envelope math, figured I could just about afford one were I to correctly wager on the underdog Raiders. By the time I arrived at the tailgate festivities, I had $1,000 on the line.

The Raiders play in Allegiant Stadium, in nearby Paradise, perhaps the most potent example of the synthesis of gambling and sport. For decades, the NFL refused to engage with Vegas, and Nevada itself remained cautious of combining sports and gambling, prohibiting the placement of wagers on all teams from within state lines “in order to avoid the slightest hint of impropriety,” according to a 1998 regulatory report. Ahead of Super Bowl XXXVII, in 2003, the league declined to air an advertisement created by the city, despite its not even featuring gambling.

But inevitably, the money became too big to pass up, and some of the NFL old guard bought in. In 2015, both Jerry Jones and Robert Kraft, the owners of the Cowboys and Patriots, respectively, had invested in DraftKings. That same year, the NFL cut a three-year deal with the organization to partner on international games held in England, where online sports betting was formally legalized in 2005. In 2017, NFL owners voted 31–1 to bring the Raiders from Oakland to Vegas. Then the Las Vegas Sands CEO Sheldon Adelson helped secure $750 million in public funds for the construction of Allegiant Stadium. Its founding partners included Boyd Gaming, Caesars Rewards, Wynn Resorts, and the Yaamava’ Resort and Casino.

This was my first time in an NFL stadium, and thus my first time betting on football in person. There was a certain thrill to being so close to the action. Plus, if you bet on an app while in the stands, you can arguably gain a slight edge (though such “courtsiding” is more common in sports like tennis, which have a larger delay in game-data transmission). The league provides rapid updates to the sports data and technology firm Genius, which feeds the information to the various apps in real time.

Settling into my seat in the nosebleeds, I met a die-hard Raiders fan and recovering gambling addict named Ted Hartwell, a mild-mannered guy with gray hair and a baritone voice. I confessed to Ted that I had spread a fair bit of money around town. He seemed concerned—not by the betting itself, but by the incompetency of my strategy.

I’d put the lion’s share of my money on the Raiders winning outright, and bet that they would score a touchdown on their first drive, as well as that Ashton Jeanty, an elite running back, would at some point find his way into the end zone. The Raiders had, in impressive form, bested the Patriots the previous week, but they were still considered to be not very good. Charitably, Ted reckoned that I would at least win the Jeanty bet. I didn’t. Instead of scoring on their opening drive, quarterback Geno Smith threw an interception.

As Jumbotron ads for BetMGM loomed above us, Ted explained his theory that gambling addiction is half genetic and half environmental. His own history strongly illustrates his point. Starting in 1976, when Ted was ten, his father, a college music teacher in Lubbock, Texas, would take him to a horse track in Ruidoso, New Mexico, on weekends. At the track, Ted would read the history of the horses and advise his dad’s bets. By eleven, he was buying scratch-offs and lottery tickets. In high school, Ted joined weekly poker games, where he eventually made enough money to move out of the house and quit his gig at Pizza Hut.

Sometime after moving to Vegas for an academic job in his twenties, Ted hit his first jackpot on a video poker machine, pocketing a thousand bucks—then got another big win less than a week later. But his losses also started to accrue, and Ted quietly rolled some of his debt into his mortgage, then began to ask for money from friends and distant family members. He secured credit cards and payday loans that he didn’t tell his wife about, all while keeping his family’s checking and savings accounts in good stead. Once Ted’s wife discovered that he was roughly $100,000 in the red, he began seeing a therapist and, on September 14, 2007, placed his last-ever bet.

Around the time he stopped gambling, a member of Ted’s family showed him 120-year-old correspondence from his great-grandfather, who was writing home to his mother. “In nearly every letter, he asks for money for multiple different reasons,” Ted told me. “That’s not proof positive that anything was going on, but it was just the way that he asked for it, and the multitude of different reasons he asked for money, that reminded me of myself.”

I reflected on my own family tree, and particularly on my dad, who is sober, but has what some would call reckless instincts. He has productively funneled them into a career in the nonprofit arts world, first as a live-theater and dance presenter, then as a producer of a kids’ circus, and later as an independent filmmaker. “I lost ten grand on Miles Davis,” my dad once told me. “But it was a great show and worth every penny to bring him to Vermont.” Years later, while shooting his fourth feature film, a Prohibition-era rum-running adventure starring Kris Kristofferson, a major source of funding evaporated days before principal photography was due to take place, leading my dad to take out a second mortgage on our house in order to fill the production’s $132,000 shortfall. He didn’t have the money to do all this—just the desire to make it happen, no matter the odds.

The rest of the football game went very poorly. Near the end, the Raiders totally wrecked any chance of a comeback with an interception in the end zone. Later, the media reported that Geno Smith had played his worst game in more than a decade.

As I held my head in my hands, Ted told me that he far prefers watching sports without money on the line. He no longer frets over the minutiae of passing yards, and can simply enjoy the pure, invigorating thrill of witnessing athletic excellence. As we left the stadium, I was feeling eczema-level itchy to recoup my losses, and Ted passed along a helpful tip given to him by a former sponsor. He said that if an addict ever finds himself in a casino, he should ignore the buzzy slot machines and focus instead on the faces of the people playing them.

One of my last stops in town was the Dr. Robert Hunter International Problem Gambling Center—I’d seen enough of gambling’s cause and wanted some sense of the effect. In the clinic’s lobby, I locked eyes with a smiling older woman. She walked over and hugged me, asking how long I’d been in recovery. I sheepishly clarified that I was a reporter. She told me that, over the course of her decades working in the casinos, she’d bet away her entire 401(k), much of it at slot machines owned by her employer. “I wish I knew about this place many years ago,” she said.

To those who knew him, Robert Hunter was “Las Vegas’s therapist,” a moniker he’d earned through his outsized presence in a place that has long ranked perilously low in public support for behavioral health. On a public radio program, one of Hunter’s protégés recalled him routinely fielding desperate mental-health and addiction queries while roaming the streets of Sin City. He had been trained to treat various addictions and had voiced TV spots for a local hospital’s innovative gambling-addiction program, where he worked. Once the hospital shuttered in 1998, Hunter founded his own nonprofit clinic. For a time, no one in the world was believed to have treated more gambling addicts than him. (Hunter died of heart failure in 2018, just two months before the Supreme Court opened the floodgates to online sports gambling.)

At his clinic, Hunter devised a compassionate six-week outpatient treatment model in which he combined the clinical approaches of cognitive behavioral therapy with motivational interviewing techniques and lectured to new clients about the biochemical underpinnings of their behavior. Subtending everything was an emphasis on group connection and community.

The last comprehensive study on the prevalence of gambling addiction in Nevada was carried out twenty-six years ago. Since then, the state’s population has grown by over a million residents. It’s estimated that only 1 percent of problem gamers secure free treatment through the state. On average, around 550 Nevadans are provided with state-sponsored specialized treatment each year. There aren’t many certified counselors, either. The chair of the state’s Advisory Committee on Problem Gambling told me that there is exactly one gambling-addiction specialist within state lines who is fluent in Spanish. A decent awareness campaign would easily burn through the entire $2 million budget allocated to deal with the issue.

Stephanie Goodman, the center’s executive director, spent the past year trying to change the state’s funding formula using a new rule that would allocate half a percent of Nevada’s gambling revenue to treatment efforts. This seems small, but it would roughly double the current budget to about $5.5 million and wouldn’t cost the casinos anything—it would simply earmark part of the state’s haul. Stephanie told me that casinos had been “vocally” supportive of the idea, as had lawmakers on both sides of the aisle, but by the time the legislative session ended in June, none had come forward to sponsor the measure.

For decades, Stephanie had worked as an advertising executive for casino clients, including the Luxor and the Plaza. Her father-in-law is Oscar Goodman, a former mob lawyer who, as the mayor of Vegas, was a pioneering advocate for the city getting its own professional sports team. Stephanie has vilified predatory slot-machine operators and is opposed to prop bets, which provide opportunities to bet on an individual play or a player statistic.

Still, she is careful when describing the industry titans from whom she would like to secure support. “We’re trying to move the needle, which is why I’m not gonna throw anybody under the bus,” she told me. Minutes later, she insisted that Circa’s CEO, Derek Stevens, “is very sympathetic to the work we do.” When I asked whether Stevens, a man with a net worth estimated to be as high as $800 million, has contributed any money to the center, she clarified that she hadn’t asked him.

Of course, things can always get worse—and my experience in Vegas convinced me that they almost certainly will. After visiting the problem-gambling center, I headed to the University of Nevada, Las Vegas, where the International Gaming Institute can be found. The institute, which some refer to as the “Harvard of gaming,” is housed in the Stan Fulton Building, named in tribute to a legendary Las Vegan and slot-machine innovator. The Dr. Robert Hunter International Problem Gambling Center occupies part of one floor in a two-story office building. IGI, meanwhile, which Fulton initially endowed with more than $6 million, is located in a three-story, 37,000-square-foot building that sits just off the Strip, on Flamingo Road.

If Vegas represented a prosocial form of betting, every technological trend seems hellbent on moving us in the opposite direction, largely by offering ever more warped, addictive, and isolating versions of the casino for our phones. Social-media companies, much like the betting apps, have taken the allure of slots to the next level: endless scrolling feeds, hyperactive alerts, and special rewards. Today, the human body is so reliant on these dopamine hits that it often sends phantom signals to the brain simulating the buzz of a phone notification. Kids are further solidifying these neural links via video games, many of which now feature “loot box” games in which players pay for randomized upgrades.

Just through the front doors of IGI is the Gaming Lab, where experts attempt to divine the future of gambling. There I met the center’s director of gaming innovation, Daniel Sahl, who told me he had come to believe that we are headed toward a more streamlined version of what he called “meaningful winning experiences.” The game he proceeded to show me on his screen was not a fancy, flashy prototype but a simple black line moving upward. The winner is the person who can hold on to this trajectory the longest, ideally cashing out in the microsecond before the randomized formula shoots the line perilously downward. Depending on your vantage point, the game either mimics an accelerated version of the stock market or a human heartbeat on a medical monitor. It’s called Crash, and I bet that one day you’ll be playing it.
